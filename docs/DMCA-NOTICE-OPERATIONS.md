## Intake and recordkeeping

1. Preserve the original notice, attachments, headers, received date and time, sender, affected URL, subscriber, and internal case notes.
2. Assign a case identifier and acknowledge receipt without deciding the claim in the acknowledgment.
3. Treat claimant and subscriber contact details as sensitive. Share them only as required for the statutory process, counsel, or lawful requests.
4. Do not delete the underlying storage object merely to disable public access. Preserve evidence and account records while the matter is active.

## Review a takedown notice

Confirm that the notice substantially includes:

- a physical or electronic signature;
- identification of the copyrighted work or a representative list;
- identification and location of the disputed material;
- claimant contact information;
- the required good-faith statement; and
- the required accuracy and authority statement under penalty of perjury.

If core information is missing but the sender identified the work, material, and contact method, ask for the missing information and document the request. Do not promise a specific legal outcome.

## Respond to a substantially compliant notice

1. Act expeditiously to remove or disable public access to the specifically identified material.
2. Promptly notify the subscriber who supplied the material. Include the case identifier, affected material, removal status, and instructions for submitting a counter-notice.
3. Forward the notice as appropriate. Warn the subscriber that claimant contact information is part of the process.
4. Record the action, time, affected URLs and objects, subscriber notice, and delivery result.
5. Add the incident to the subscriber's infringement history for repeat-infringer review.

## Review a counter-notice

Confirm that the counter-notice substantially includes:

- the subscriber's physical or electronic signature;
- identification and former location of the removed material;
- the good-faith mistake or misidentification statement under penalty of perjury;
- the subscriber's name, address, and phone number; and
- consent to the appropriate federal-court jurisdiction and acceptance of service of process.

Promptly forward a substantially compliant counter-notice to the original claimant and record the transmission time.

## Restoration window

After forwarding a compliant counter-notice, do not restore the material before 10 business days. Restore it no later than 14 business days unless the claimant first provides notice of a filed court action seeking to restrain the subscriber's allegedly infringing activity. Confirm unusual filings with counsel before restoring or permanently disabling access.

## Repeat infringers

Review the complete history rather than relying only on a raw notice count. In appropriate circumstances, terminate or restrict subscribers who are repeat infringers. Record the facts, decisions, warnings, restrictions, and termination rationale consistently. Do not ignore red-flag infringement merely because no formal notice arrived.

## Subpoenas, emergencies, and escalation

- Route subpoenas, court orders, law-enforcement demands, and threatened litigation to counsel immediately.
- Preserve relevant account and access records when a dispute is reasonably anticipated.
- Escalate notices involving fair use, ownership disputes, licensed material, impersonation, privacy, explicit content, threats, or many accounts.
- Never disclose subscriber identity solely because a claimant asks; require valid legal process when applicable.

## Registration and renewal

The public policy and this procedure do not replace the federal designation. Register the Copyright Agent in the U.S. Copyright Office's online DMCA Designated Agent Directory. Calendar renewal reminders at 120, 90, 60, and 30 days before the three-year expiration and update the designation promptly whenever the legal name, alternate names, agent, address, phone, or email changes.
